Chemistry — States of Matter

States of Matter & Changes of State

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

🧊 SOLID

📦Particles packed tightly in a fixed, regular arrangement
🔒Cannot flow or be compressed
📐Keeps its own shape and volume
🔊Particles vibrate but cannot move around

💧 LIQUID

🌊Particles close but can move past each other
🫙Takes the shape of its container
📏Keeps the same volume
💨Can flow but cannot be compressed

☁️ GAS

💨Particles far apart with very weak forces between them
🫧Fills any container — no fixed shape or volume
⚡Moves fast in all directions
🗜️Can be compressed (squashed)

🔄 Changes of State

🔥Melting — solid → liquid (heat)
❄️Freezing — liquid → solid (cool)
♨️Evaporation/Boiling — liquid → gas (heat)
🌫️Condensation — gas → liquid (cool)
✨Sublimation — solid → gas directly
Water melts at 0°C  ·  Water boils at 100°C
💡 Remember: physical changes don't make new substances — mass stays the same!

Physics — Electrical Circuits

Electrical Circuits

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

⚡ Key Definitions

⚡Current — flow of electrical charge around a circuit. Measured in amps (A)
🔋Voltage — the push that drives current around. Measured in volts (V)
🌀Resistance — how much a component opposes current. Measured in ohms (Ω)
🔄Current is NOT used up as it flows — it is always the same around a circuit

🔗 Series vs Parallel

1️⃣Series — one single loop. If one bulb breaks, ALL go out
🔀Parallel — separate branches. If one bulb breaks, others STAY ON
🏠Your home uses parallel circuits — that's why one light going off doesn't turn off everything

📏 Measuring Tools

🅰️Ammeter — measures current in amps. Connected in SERIES
🅥Voltmeter — measures voltage in volts. Connected in PARALLEL

🔣 Circuit Symbols

🔋Battery — two lines (long +, short −)
💡Bulb — circle with X inside
🔘Switch open — broken line; closed — complete line
🅰️Ammeter — circle with A; Voltmeter — circle with V
Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current  ·  R = V ÷ I
💡 Think of voltage like water pressure and current like the flow of water — the battery is the pump!

Physics — Energy

Energy Stores & Transfers

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

🔋 The 6 Energy Stores

⚡Kinetic — energy of movement. Faster = more
🍎Gravitational potential — stored due to height. Higher = more
🌡️Thermal — stored as heat in a substance
🥗Chemical — stored in food, fuel, batteries
🌀Elastic potential — stored in stretched/squashed objects
⚛️Nuclear — stored in the nucleus of atoms

🔄 Energy Transfers

🔑Energy is never created or destroyed — only transferred from one store to another
💡Torch: chemical → electrical → light + thermal
🎢Roller coaster: gravitational potential → kinetic
🏹Bow and arrow: elastic potential → kinetic

♻️ Renewable Resources

✅Will NOT run out
☀️Solar, wind, tidal, hydroelectric, geothermal, biomass
🌍Most originally come from the Sun's energy

🏭 Non-Renewable Resources

❌Will run out one day
⛽Coal, oil, natural gas (fossil fuels), nuclear
🦕Fossil fuels took millions of years to form from dead plants and animals
Law of Conservation of Energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed

Chemistry — Particle Theory

Particle Theory, Diffusion & Brownian Motion

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

⚛️ The Big Idea

🔬All materials are made of tiny particles — too small to see
📦Solid — particles tightly packed, fixed position, vibrate only
💧Liquid — particles close, can move past each other, flow
☁️Gas — particles far apart, move fast in all directions

🌡️ Temperature & Particles

🔥Heat a substance → particles gain energy → move faster
📏Particles spread further apart → substance expands
🌡️This explains how thermometers work — liquid expands when heated

💨 Diffusion

👃Particles spread from where there are lots of them to where there are fewer
🌸Example: perfume spreads across a room
🔑Moves from high concentration → low concentration
♨️Faster at higher temperatures — particles have more energy

🎲 Brownian Motion

🎱The random movement of particles suspended in a liquid or gas
💥Caused by collisions with other invisible particles
🔭First seen with pollen grains in water under a microscope
🧠Einstein explained it in 1905 — proved atoms exist!
Diffusion: HIGH concentration → LOW concentration (particles spread out)

Chemistry — Separating Mixtures

Chromatography

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

🎨 What is Chromatography?

🔍A technique to separate mixtures — especially inks and dyes
🖊️For example — is a black pen ink one colour or many mixed together?
👮Used in crime labs to identify unknown substances!

🧪 How It Works

📄Stationary phase = the paper — it stays still
💧Mobile phase = the solvent (water) — it moves UP the paper
🏃More soluble substances travel FURTHER up the paper
⬆️The starting spot must be ABOVE the solvent level — or it washes away

📊 Reading Results

1️⃣One spot = pure substance — it doesn't separate
🔢Multiple spots = a mixture — each component travels differently
🔄Same Rf value = likely the same substance

📐 Rf Value

📏Rf = distance substance travels ÷ distance solvent travels
🔢Always between 0 and 1
🆔Use Rf values to identify unknown substances by comparing to known ones
Rf = distance substance travels ÷ distance solvent travels
💡 Remember: stationary = stays still (paper), mobile = moves (solvent/water)

Chemistry — Solubility

Solubility

📖 Read This First
New to this topic? Read through before you practise.

🧂 Key Words

✅Soluble — dissolves in a solvent (e.g. salt in water)
❌Insoluble — does NOT dissolve (e.g. sand in water)
🧂Solute — the substance that dissolves (e.g. salt)
💧Solvent — the liquid doing the dissolving (e.g. water)
🫙Solution — the mixture formed (e.g. saltwater)

🌡️ Temperature & Solubility

🔥Hotter water = more solid dissolves (usually)
☕Example: more sugar dissolves in hot tea than cold water
🛑Saturated solution — no more solute can dissolve at that temperature

🔄 Reversible Change

🔬Dissolving is a physical change — no new substance is made
↩️It can be reversed by evaporation — heat the solution, water evaporates, solid left behind
🧂This is how sea salt is made — evaporate seawater!

🔬 Scientific Enquiry

📊To compare solubility: use same amount of solvent, same temperature, same stirring
⚖️Add measured amounts of solute until no more dissolves = find solubility
📈Plot a solubility curve — shows how solubility changes with temperature
Solute + Solvent → Solution  ·  (e.g. Salt + Water → Saltwater)
💡 Memory trick: the SOLute diSOLves in the SOLvent to make a SOLution — all start with SOL!
